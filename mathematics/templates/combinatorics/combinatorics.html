Combinatorics

Let's start with something easy.

Example 1:

Suppose you have a drawer filled with 10 socks — 5 black and 5 white. How many socks must you pull out of the drawer to guarantee a match?

Example 1 is an application of the pigeonhole principle, which is defined as follows:

If there are n items and m containers, with n > m, then at least one container contains two items.
Suppose we have 2 containers, one labeled black and the other white. When we pull a black sock from the drawer, it will go in the container labeled black and a white sock will go into the container labeled white.

Suppose further that the first selection is black and the second is white. Each container now contains a sock. If the third selection is black, we have two black socks and one container (black), forcing a pair in that container. A white sock would also force a pair in the white container.

Example 2:

Eddy is in a room with 13 co-workers. Having recently learned the pigeonhole principle, he smugly informs one of them, Benjamin, that at least one of his co-workers must share the same birthday month as himself — February.

Benjamin kindly informs him that he is mistaken. Eddy chuckles at the ignorance of his co-worker and light-heartedly calls everyone over. He takes a poll of all thirteen of his coworkers. One by one they list their birthday months, and to his growing dismay, not one of them was born in February.

Red-faced and defeated, Eddy sulks over to a corner of the room. What happened? Why had the pigeonhole principle failed him?









Benjamin walks over the cabinet, pulls out 12 coffee cups and thirteen spoons and places them in front of Eddy.

"That's a common mistake. See these coffee cups? If each of these cups represents a different month of the year and each of these spoons represents one of the people in this room, then we can place a spoon in a given cup if that person was born in the month represented by the cup.

For arguments sake, suppose everyone except me has a different birth month. That means one spoon will be distributed to each cup. My birthday is in October. The October cup will be the only one that contains two spoons. February—your birth month—still contains you alone. Make sense?"

Eddy nodded slightly and grumbled something about it being Tuesday today and not Monday.

Poker Probabilities

Let's move on to a more challenging topic—calculating the probability of some of the hands in a poker game.

Experiment with these probabilities → here ← before continuing.

We'll use a standard 52-card deck (no Jokers).
Here are a few more concepts we'll need to analyze these problems completely:

Combinations
The number of possible ways to choose k items from a group of size n is given by the equation:

C(n,k) = n!/((n-k)!*k!)

where C(n,k) is read as "n choose k"—the number of unique combinations possible if items are chosen k at a time from a group of size n—and n! = n*(n-1)*(n-2)...*2*1 is is read "n factorial" (e.g. 5! = 5*4*3*2*1 = 120).

The order of the items does not matter in a combination:
{1,2,3} is the same as {3,2,1}

Multiplication Principle
The multiplication principle states:

If there are N1 items in set 1 and N2 items in set 2, then there are N1* N2 ways to combine the items from set 1 and set 2.

(e.g. given 3 subjects, 4 verbs, and 8 predicates, there are 3 * 4 * 8 = 96 unique sentences that can be formed, assuming only one sentence part can be chosen from each category for any given sentence.)

The number of possible 5-card hands is given by the equation:

C(52,5) = 52!/(47!*5!) = 2,598,960 poker hands

This number is important and will be used in most of our calculations.

Calculating the Probability of a Pair

Part 1
How unique pairs exist in a 52-card deck (where order does not matter)?








For any given card value, there are four suits. These suits can be paired up in C(4,2) = 6 ways.

There are 13 card values; each of these has 6 possible pairs. The total number of pairs possible in a 52-card deck is:

C(4,2) * C(13,1) = 6 * 13 = 78 pairs
Part 2
If a given pair has already been chosen, how many combinations of the other three cards are possible?
(No other pairs, such as two pair or three-of-a-kind, are allowed.)








Since a three-of-a-kind is not allowed, the other two cards of the same value as the pair cannot be used. That leaves 12 remaining card values.

There are C(12,3) ways to choose 3 (unique) values from the 12. Each of these values has 4 possible suits. This multiplies every combination of 3 card-values by 4*4*4 = 64 (multiplication principle). For example, {3, J, K} is not one combination but 64, corresponding to { {3 Spades, J Spades, K Spades}, {3 Spades, J Spades, K Hearts}, ... }.

The total number of 3-card combinations given that a pair has already been chosen is:

C(12,3) * 4 * 4 * 4 = 220 * 64 = 14,080 combinations
Part 3
Using the answers from Parts 1 and 2, we can determine that the probability of having a pair is:








Part 2 tells us there are 14,080 possible hands that contain a given pair, and Part 1 that there are 78 possible pairs.

By the multiplication principle, there are:

14,080 * 78 = 1,098,240 hands that contain a pair.

The probability of getting a pair (assuming any hand is equally likely) is:

(1,098,240 5-card hands with pairs) / (2,598,960 5-card hands) ≈ .42

Calculating the Probability of a Two Pair

Follow the same steps as the previous problem to calculate the probability of a two-pair.

You can use the results of the experiment page to confirm your calculations.

The probability of a two-pair is:








There are C(13,2) = 78 ways to choose two values from the thirteen. Each value has C(4,2) = 6 pairs possible (so we must multiply by C(4,2) twice). The number of two-pairs possible is:

C(13,2) * C(4,2) * C(4,2) = 78 * 6 * 6 = 2808 two-pairs.

The number of ways to choose the last card from the remaining card values (which—to avoid the formation of other hands like a full-house—must be chose from the values not yet used) is:

(# of remaining values) * (# of suits) = C(11,1) * C(4,1) = 11 * 4 = 44

The number of hands with a two-pair is:

C(13,2) * C(4,2) * C(4,2) * C(11,1) * C(4,1) = 78 * 6 * 6 * 11 * 4 = 123,352 hands

Therefore, the probability of a two-pair is:

(123,352 hands with a two-pair) / (2,598,960 hands) ≈ .048

Calculating the Probability of a Three-of-a-Kind

Last one.

The probability of a three-of-a-kind is:









There are C(13,1) = 13 ways to choose a value from the thirteen. Each value has C(4,3) = 4 three-of-a-kinds possible.

The number of three-of-a-kinds possible is:

C(13,1) * C(4,3) = 13 * 4 = 52 three-of-a-kinds.

The number of ways to choose the last two cards from the remaining card values is:

C(12,2) * C(4,1) * C(4,1) = 66 * 4 * 4 = 1056

The number of hands with a three-of-a-kind is:

C(13,1) * C(4,3) * C(12,2) * C(4,1) * C(4,1) = 13 * 4 * 66 * 4 * 4 = 54,912 hands

Therefore, the probability of a three-of-a-kind is:

(54,912 hands with a two-pair) / (2,598,960 hands) ≈ .021
